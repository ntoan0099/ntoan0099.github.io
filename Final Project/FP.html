Discussion

Through my conversation with Carlamere, I notice her literacy—her love, her passion for the writings of the arts, stem mostly from her father. As she mentions in her retelling of her backstory, Carlamere speaks fondly of her dad introducing her to many wonders of literacy in the form of movies. He exposed her to world of cinema.

“I like to think of myself as a storyteller. So I always like see my dad as like the person who got me into that. So like storytelling-wise, like also like TV I guess is before writing or reading, but then like once I got into reading stories and writing my own stories, my dad is the person I see as like what got me into that world. I think so,” says Carlamere when asked about her first-hand experience with finding her literacy. In some shapes or form, her father implicitly guided her toward a career where she enjoyed deeply. Carlamere also mentions about television helped shape her ambition into reality—like she announces to herself, “A storyteller.”

Carlamere’s backstory with her literacy confirms my intuition: our literacy is dictated by our surroundings, especially by our family.

Carlemere comments, “So I definitely read books when I was younger, but where like, most of the stories I consumed were always, like, TV shows and movies, especially because of my dad and it was, like, something we did together and something we bonded over.” By having deep social interaction (in this case, a father-daughter activity), literacy is spread from one to another. Her dad’s interests in films and entertainment transcended over to her, leading Carlemere to explore her passion in cinema, more importantly, being a storyteller.

As mentioned in Deborah Brandt’s essay “Sponsors of Literacy,” “Sponsors are delivery systems for the economies of literacy, the means by which these forces present themselves to—and through—individual learners. They also represent the causes into which people’s literacy usually get recruited”

(Brandt 167).Brandt, Deborah. “Sponsors of Literacy.” College Composition and Communication, vol. 49, no. 2, 1998, pp. 165–85. JSTOR, https://doi.org/10.2307/358929. Accessed 18 Dec. 2024.

Being a “sponsor” of literacy increases the chance of individual inheriting values and beliefs from certain practices and learning. In the case of Carlemere, she inherited her passion of films through her father. Her exposure to creatives propelled to find her passion in life, and it was opened by her “sponsor”—her father.

Carlemere’s father’s influence of literacy even dated back to her early days as a toddler, asking what did certain words mean. However, during the process of asking her dad for the meaning of each word, her father acted as a gatekeeper of information. Thus, he implicitly presented himself as a delivery system for literacy.

Furthermore, into our conversation, Carlemere reflects back on her journey with writing, from the early years of elementary school to early college. At the beginning, Carlemere showed a high passion for creative writing, yet into years of analytical writing, the school institution slowly killed her passion, making her fell out of it. Until she got into the years of college where her attitude revealed a sudden change—she liked doing both analytical and creative (they both grant her a sense of enjoyment with writing). The result of her enjoying writing more is an unexpected outcome. I was strongly believed that her attitude about essay writing would like a chore, yet she deemed them fun to write about.

Carlemere expresses desires to pursue more creative works with her writing skills. She wants to work in publishing; however, her real passion is to hone in her screenwriting ability.

According to “Nutrition, Health, and Wellness at La Escuelaita: A Community-Driven Effort Toward Food and Environmental Justice,” “WE have found food not only acts as a common language, but as Rick Flowers and Elaine Sawn noted in their work on food pedagogy, it enables learning”

(Del Hierro et al., 31).Del Hierro, Victor, et al. "Nutrition, Health, and Wellness at La Escuelita: A Community-Driven Effort Toward Food and Environmental Justice." Community Literacy Journal, vol. 14 no. 1, 2019, p. 26-43. Project MUSE, https://dx.doi.org/10.1353/clj.2019.0023.

The authors of the article might refer to literacy to food, but it is no difference from films. They are visual symbols that transcend meaning and it is a universal language. To Carlemere, films and stories are her language to understand more about herself and the world.

Granted, she barely has any social media influence acted upon her literacy, but with Reddit being her community for ideas, Carlemere participated in literacy exchange. Just like food, creative ideas that Carlemere possesses are universal language for creators—they are visions for fantasy and fable. Those creative ideas are the way for Carlemere to spread her values and beliefs, becoming a sponsor for other to adapt and learn.

In some ways, Carlemere’s journey concludes the fact that school writing and educational institutions doesn’t really change the what school writing is, but the attitude is shifted due to a higher level of freedom being given. Thus, with more creative decision going into writing and text, school writing becomes less of a chore and more engaging to write about.

Just like Doctor Jackie Regan, co-founder and co-chair of Text, Power, Telling, Carlemere uses her writing to express an idea, a feeling—even though it might be fictional stories, they still carry a vital message. Carlemere instills her values into her writing, and later on publish them to spread her ideologies to society. Granted, her stories might not be about overcoming sexual abuse, but they infuse with meaning that can help change lives. Stories are powerful in their own ways with their own fable and themes. Ultimately, Carlemere’s literacy is through the medium of storytelling, but they pack with life-lesson potency as she experiences new traditions and feelings through stories. By writing her stories, Carlemere curates a narrative that are filled with her background knowledge and pre-existing values; later, those stories will be spread throughout the public, leading to them learning new values and beliefs, incepted by Carlemere herself.

Looking Beyond

Through my understanding of Carlemere’s literacy and her perspective on it, I gather that literacy is an exchange of ideas and understanding. Yes, there is still writing and reading involved, but it is more about how information is consumed and adapted. With the study of Carlemere, I deduce the fact that literacy is not embedded only in text, but rather all sort of mediums—from sign and symbols to color visuals. However, there are some limitations regarding the case study with it being a one-person study.

By having only one sample, the margin of error to form an absolute conclusion is challenging. With many people experience different levels of obtaining literacy. Additionally, most people coin the term “literacy” with association of text, so having different subjects would result in multiple ways to identify what is literacy. However, the question still remains Is literacy obtained through means of text or exchanges of communications and ideas?

In order to answer this question, a study of two group, one group will be given text materials to understand, while the other is offered discussion and visual aids. At the end of the observation period, the two groups will test how much they learn about the topic. Ultimately, the outcome would be astounding varies since everyone has different experiences with the content, but nonetheless, the margin of errors will narrow, resulting a more concrete conclusion about what exactly is literacy and how we define it.

Overall, there is no concrete way to measure literacy except learning them through people’s lens. In some way, literacy is still a theoretical idea that appear in many forms. The way people interpret its meaning can be varies from its being definition that associated with text, or some other shapes—literacy is subjective. Everyone has a different opinion. Thus, literacy can never be fully defined by textbook, but in one way or another, everyone has their meaning on literacy and what it means to them.
